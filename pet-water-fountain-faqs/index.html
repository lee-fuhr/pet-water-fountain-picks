Pet Water Fountain questions answered

Updated June 2026Independently researchedNo paid placement.

Quick answer

Pet water fountains encourage cats and dogs to drink more by providing a constant flow of filtered, fresh-tasting water. They are especially helpful for pets prone to urinary issues, but choosing the right model and staying on top of cleaning are key to keeping your pet healthy and hydrated.

Do pets really need a water fountain?

Not every pet needs a fountain, but many benefit from one. Cats in particular have a low thirst drive and evolved to prefer moving water, a fountain taps into that instinct. Dogs with flat faces, heavy droolers, or senior joints may also drink more from a gently circulating source. The biggest advantage is hydration. A fountain’s continuous flow keeps water oxygenated and free of the stale, dusty taste that can build up in a bowl. Urinary tract health, kidney function, and even dental health can improve when a pet drinks more. That said, a clean, frequently refreshed bowl works perfectly fine for many pets. A fountain is a convenience and an incentive, not a necessity.

How often should I clean a pet water fountain?

Most manufacturers and experienced owners recommend a full cleaning every three to seven days. The exact interval depends on the number of pets, how much dust or fur floats around, and whether the fountain sits in a sunny spot. Hard-water deposits and biofilm (a slippery slime) can form surprisingly fast, especially in plastic fountains. A thorough clean means disassembling the pump, scrubbing the reservoir and spout with a soft brush and mild soap (avoid abrasive pads that create scratch-harboring surfaces), and rinsing everything well. Many owners also run a diluted vinegar or enzymatic cleaner through the pump at least once a month to remove buildup from hard water or saliva. Skipping cleanings leads to clogged pumps, foul smells, and, more importantly, a pet that suddenly rejects the water.

What should I look for when buying a pet water fountain?

Start with the material. Stainless steel is easiest to keep hygienic, most durable, and dishwasher-safe &#8212; it doesn’t develop the micro-scratches that trap bacteria like plastic can. Ceramic looks nice and is heavy (less tipping) but is more breakable. BPA-free plastic is budget-friendly but needs replacement every year or two as the surface degrades. Next, consider the filtration. Most fountains come with a replaceable carbon cartridge that removes chlorine, bad tastes, and odors. Some add an ion-exchange resin or a foam pre-filter to catch hair and debris. Make sure replacement filters are easy to find and reasonably priced, because you’ll be buying them often. Capacity matters: a one-gallon reservoir suits a single cat for several days; multiple pets or large breeds may require a gallon and a half or more. Noise level is also key, pumps with a ceramic impeller or a submersible design tend to be quieter than cheap spinning systems. Finally, check whether the pump is easy to fully disassemble for cleaning; a sealed pump unit is a headache waiting to happen.

Are pet water fountains noisy?

The short answer is: some are, most aren’t, and it largely depends on design and maintenance. A well-made fountain with a submerged pump and a wide, gentle spillway will run nearly silently, you might hear a soft trickle but no motor hum. Cheap fountains with underpowered pumps or narrow water channels often create a louder splashing or whirring sound. Noise changes as a fountain gets dirty. A partial clog in the pump or mineral buildup around the impeller can turn a quiet unit into a grumbly one. Regular cleaning restores the original volume. Also, the water level matters: if the pump ever gulps air because the reservoir is low, you’ll hear it. Most owners report that after a brief adjustment period, the sound of moving water becomes white noise they barely notice.

How do I get my cat or dog to use a water fountain?

Start by placing the fountain near their regular water station but leave the old bowl out for a couple of days so they can approach the new one on their own terms. Cats are especially cautious about unfamiliar objects, moving water may spook them at first. Run the fountain on the gentlest flow setting and let them see you fill it or splash your finger in it. For a stubborn pet, try wiping a tiny bit of tuna juice (for cats) or chicken broth (unsalted, no onion) around the rim. Never force them to drink from it; most pets will investigate once they see the water moving or hear the trickle. Once they do, remove the old bowl gradually. If your pet still avoids the fountain after a week, check whether the pump noise is bothering them or if the water feels too cold, ceramic and metal fountains can chill water on cool surfaces.

What kind of filter does a pet water fountain need?

The most common filter is a replaceable carbon cartridge that uses activated charcoal to pull out chlorine, bad tastes, and odors. That alone makes tap water more appealing. Many fountains stack a white foam pad on top of the carbon, that foam catches hair, kibble crumbs, and other large debris before they reach the pump. Some higher-end models add an ion-exchange resin that softens hard water by trapping calcium and magnesium ions. This reduces white scaling on the fountain and may be easier on sensitive kidneys, but it also means the filter packs need replacement more often. A few fountains omit carbon entirely and rely on a ceramic disc or an ultraviolet light, these are very niche. For most people, a simple carbon-plus-foam combo is perfectly effective and the cheapest to maintain.

Frequently asked questions

Is it safe to leave a pet water fountain running all day?

Yes, modern fountains are designed for continuous operation. Most pumps use low-voltage DC power and include an automatic shut-off if the water level drops too low. As long as you keep the reservoir filled and the pump clean, leaving it on 24/7 is perfectly safe and even recommended to encourage drinking.

Can I put a pet water fountain in the dishwasher?

It depends on the material. Stainless-steel fountains are almost always dishwasher-safe (top rack recommended). Ceramic fountains often are, but check the manufacturer’s note, some glazes can crack or fade over time. Plastic fountains should never go in the dishwasher; the high heat can warp parts and cause BPA leaching if the plastic isn’t food-grade. The pump itself is never dishwasher-safe.

Do pet water fountains use a lot of electricity?

No. Most fountain pumps draw between 1.5 and 5 watts, less than a cell-phone charger. Running a fountain 24/7 typically costs less than a few dollars per year on an average electric bill. Some cordless, battery-powered models exist, but they require frequent charging and aren’t practical for continuous use.

Why does my pet water fountain get slimy so fast?

That slimy biofilm is a mix of bacteria, saliva, and minerals. It forms fastest in plastic fountains because the surface has microscopic scratches where microbes cling. Switching to stainless steel or ceramic and cleaning every three days will drastically reduce the slime. Also, avoid putting the fountain in direct sunlight, which accelerates microbial growth.

How do I know if the pump is failing?

Common signs: the fountain suddenly becomes loud or grindy, the water flow weakens or stops, or the pump makes a clicking or rattling sound. First, disassemble and clean the pump thoroughly (mineral buildup is the top cause of pump failure). If that doesn’t fix it, the impeller or motor may be worn. Replacement pumps are widely available for most popular brands.

Should I use distilled or filtered water in a pet fountain?

Ordinary tap water is fine for most fountains. If your tap water is very hard, using filtered tap water (from a pitcher or faucet filter) can reduce mineral scaling on the pump and reservoir. Distilled water is unnecessary and can actually be slightly acidic, which over time may affect metal components. Avoid adding any treatments or flavors to the water.

In shortA pet water fountain can be a real upgrade for a picky drinker, but it’s not a set-it-and-forget-it gadget. Regular cleaning, the right filter, and a material that matches your household routine make the difference between a fountain your pet loves and one that gathers dust. Start with a simple, mid-range model, see how your pet reacts, and adjust from there. The goal is more water drunk, whichever way you achieve that is a win.
